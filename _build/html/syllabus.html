The New School#

Eugene Lang College | Mathematics and Natural Sciences#

LMTH 2040

Calculus: A Multidisciplinary Approach

CRN 11022

Monday and Wednesday, 12:00 PM - 1:40 PM W11th St Room 464

  • Instructor: Jacob Frias Koehler

  • Office Hours: Wednesdays 2PM - 3PM

  • Email: koehlerj@newschool.edu

This course approaches topics in calculus through a multidisciplinary lens. It is intended for students who are new to calculus and/or are interested in a contextual application-based approach to calculus. Topics may include summations and quadrature, integration and differentiation related through the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, optimization, and basic differential equations that model natural phenomena. The course will apply a variety of approaches to explore the topics, possibly including historical and creative lenses. Students will gain skills in communicating technical and scientific information, individually and in groups, by explaining mathematical ideas in oral, written, and visual form. The course will utilize technology and mathematical software to compute and explore mathematics. This course is offered every semester and fulfills the second mathematics requirement for the Interdisciplinary Science major. This course does not fulfill the mathematics requirement for the Economics major.

Prerequisites: No Prerequisites

Co-Requisites: No Co-requisites

Learning Outcomes#

  • Use functions to model real world data

  • Use integration to find the area under a curve

  • Apply the definite and indefinite integral to data to understand probability

  • Use summations to understand data through measures of center and spread

  • Use summations and definite integrals to explore inequality, regression, and classification - problems

  • Use finite differences to explore rate of change of discrete data

  • Use derivatives to understand change in continuous data

  • Use derivatives to build regression models

  • Approximate solutions to differential equations

  • Use gradient descent to maximize or minimize functions

  • Apply gradient descent to regression and classification problems

  • Solve constrained optimization problems

  • Use constrained optimization to model data and develop conceptual economic models

Part 2: Course Outline#

  • Week 1: Foundational Problems

    • Square roots, Area under parabolas, Binomial Probability

  • Week 2: Summations

    • Sums of Integer Sequences and Proof

  • Week 3: Functions and Area

    • Polynomial, Trigonometric, and Exponential Functions, Riemann Approximations

  • Week 4: The Definite Integral

    • Definition of Definite Integral, application to area and volume

  • Week 5: Slope and Linearization

    • Slope and Linear Functions, Linearizing Polynomial and Trigonometric Functions

  • Week 6: Optimization

    • Shapes of Curves, Maximum and Minimum problems, second derivative test

  • Week 7: Probability and Statistics

    • Binomial and Normal Probability Distributions, Applications to maximum likelihood estimation and probability density and cumulative density functions, Linear and Logistic Regression

  • Week 8: Economics

    • Marginals and Game Theory, Apply derivatives to marginal analysis and use optimization to solve Nash equlibrium problems

  • Week 9: Physics

    • Motion, Work, and Force

  • Week 10: Artificial Intelligence

    • Regression and Classification Revisited, Automatic Differentiation

  • Week 11: Ordinary Differential Equations

    • Slope fields and qualitative analysis, analytic and numerical solutions

  • Week 12: Population Models

    • Linear, Exponential, and Logistic Growth Models, Systems of Differential Equations and the Lotka Volterra model

  • Week 13: Partial Differential Equations

    • The Heat and Wave Equations, Numerical solutions

  • Week 14: Final Projects

    • Final Project Proposals

  • Week 15: Final Presentations

    • Final Project Presentations

Attendance#

You are expected to attend all sessions and participate actively in the group problem solving activities. Missing more than 3 classes will result in a letter grade for each additional absence. You are expected to be on time to class, and if you are later than attendance is taken you will be marked absent.

Course Readings, Materials, and Technology Requirements#

  • Readings: We will not use a single text for this class outside of my own notes that follow. From time to time I may post additional readings in canvas, and suggest additional resources in my notes for each particular section.

  • Materials: Materials will be shared as Jupyter notebooks. These are freely available to install and use locally through Anaconda and available through your google drive as Google Colab Notebooks. All materials will be made available through both downloadable files in my github repository as well as linked as Google Colab notebooks.

  • Technology Requirements: You need a working computer that has consistent internet access and reasonable memory for using materials locally or through web access.
